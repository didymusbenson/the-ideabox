Good question, and this is where the real law can actually make the story sharper, not just more accurate.

What real protections exist

The core mechanism is two simple bureaucratic acts most tenants do on day one without thinking about it: registering their move-in (전입신고) and getting a fixed-date stamp on the contract (확정일자). Together these create repayment priority if the landlord goes bankrupt, and the lease-registration order and HUG insurance can repay the tenant if the landlord defaults. On top of that, since 2013 there's a state-backed guarantee corporation, HUG, that will pay out a tenant's jeonse deposit on the landlord's behalf if the tenant hasn't gotten the money back within a month of the contract ending, and HUG then pursues the landlord to recover it.

So in the real world, a well-informed tenant with the right paperwork isn't nearly as exposed as the premise implies — which is actually a great story lever, not a flaw:

- Halmoni Seo is exactly the kind of tenant who might not have filed the paperwork correctly, or doesn't understand what 확정일자 even is — she trusted the relationship, not the registry. That's realistic and it's why she's vulnerable in a system that technically protects people who know how to use it.
- Da-eun, the sharp younger tenant, almost certainly did everything right and has priority repayment rights on paper — meaning her actual leverage against Tae-sung and the Office is much stronger than the others'. She could be the one tenant who technically doesn't need to gamble at all, and chooses to anyway (for the investigation, or to protect the others) — which says a lot about her character.
- HUG insurance not covering everyone is your best plot-legitimate reason the syndicate exists at all. HUG has caps, waiting periods, and — critically — cases the corporation disputes or delays (contested valuations, buildings where the mortgage-to-deposit ratio makes HUG unwilling to pay quickly). The Management Office could specifically prey on tenants who fell through the HUG gap: too much owed, building overleveraged, claim under dispute. That's a real, still-current failure mode of the system (it's literally what drove the 2022–2023 jeonse fraud scandals), so it gives your syndicate a legitimate niche instead of needing tenants to be implausibly ignorant of their rights.

So — why would a legally-protected tenant get involved in an underground gambling syndicate instead of just filing a HUG claim?

That's your real motivation question, and I think the strongest version isn't "they don't know their rights," it's "their rights are too slow, too partial, or too contested for their actual crisis":
