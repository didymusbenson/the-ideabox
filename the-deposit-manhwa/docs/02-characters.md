# Characters

## Oh Tae-sung — Protagonist
31, inherited landlord, former mid-tier finance analyst who quit to "manage the building properly" and immediately proved he couldn't. Charismatic in a way that reads as sincerity right up until it doesn't. Not a cartoon villain — he genuinely liked his tenants, remembers their names, brought the noodle-shop owner soup when she was sick — which is exactly why his betrayal cuts as hard as it does. His arc is the spine of the book: from a man optimizing for his own survival to one who starts optimizing for repair.

**Flaw:** conflict-avoidant to the point of dishonesty. He didn't lie to his tenants out of malice — he lied because he couldn't stand telling them the truth, and that cowardice is what let the hole get as deep as it did.

**Skillset in-game:** unnervingly good at reading people (leftover from finance sales work), poor at raw nerve — he chokes under direct physical/psychological pressure, which the story uses to force him into partnerships rather than solo heroics.

## Kang Mi-jin — Unit 302 — *the speed gap*
Late 30s, single mother, works two jobs, the deposit is her son's spinal surgery fund parked safely (she thought) until his hospital date. Crucially: **she filed everything correctly.** 전입신고, 확정일자, all of it — her 우선변제권 is airtight, and a HUG claim would very likely pay out. The problem is timing, not entitlement: the surgery date is fixed, HUG's process isn't, and no legitimate channel can compress months of claims processing into weeks. The Management Office doesn't recruit her with a lie about her rights — it recruits her with an accurate, cruel observation about the gap between "you'll get your money" and "you'll get it in time."

The emotional gravity well of the story — every game she's in raises the stakes ceiling for everyone. Sharp, exhausted, and furious in a controlled way that's scarier than shouting. Refuses to be a symbol of pure victimhood — she is also ruthless in the games once she's in them, and the story lets her be good at winning without softening her.

## Halmoni Seo (서할머니) — Ground floor noodle shop — *the paperwork gap*
70s, rented the ground-floor commercial unit from Tae-sung's *father* for fifteen years, trusted the son by extension. Her deposit sits in a real legal gray zone: commercial leases aren't covered by the residential Housing Lease Protection Act the same way apartment units are, and fifteen years of an informal, handshake-based relationship with the previous landlord means her registry paperwork was never brought current the way a newer tenant's would be.

She isn't financially illiterate — she's run a small business's books for forty years — she simply trusted a *relationship* to do the legal work that only a filing can actually do, and by the time she understands the distinction it may be too late to fix retroactively. That's the specific injustice her arc sits on: competence in one domain (running a shop) doesn't transfer to competence in another (tenant registry law), and the system offers no grace period for the difference.

Becomes an unlikely tactical genius in games built around patience and long-con thinking — she's spent forty years reading customers and suppliers.

---

## Yoon Da-eun — Unit 501 — *the dispute gap (and the one who doesn't need to be here)*
Late 20s, webtoon artist, was already suspicious of Tae-sung's finances before the collapse (she does freelance illustration work for financial crime awareness campaigns — the irony isn't lost on her). Her paperwork is flawless and her claim is technically strong — but Tae-sung's building is overleveraged across all eleven units, meaning HUG can legally contest or slow-walk her payout rather than approve it outright, since the guarantee corporation would be absorbing part of the shortfall itself.

So she's stuck in the one gap that isn't about a tenant's mistake or bad luck: a structurally sound claim, held hostage by the fact that there genuinely isn't enough money in the building to cover everyone, and HUG knows it. That's what makes her different from Mi-jin and Halmoni Seo — she isn't playing because she's uninformed or up against a clock, she's playing because she's *correctly identified that the legitimate system has no fast answer for a building this underwater*, and decided to get answers herself instead of waiting on a dispute process with no clear timeline.

The investigator of the group; she's the one who eventually uncovers the Management Office's real business model. Becomes Tae-sung's most dangerous adversary *and*, uneasily, his most useful ally, because she's the only one thinking two levels above the games themselves.

---

## Director Baek — The Management Office
Unreadable, courteous, speaks almost entirely in real-estate and financial euphemism ("settlement," "liquidity event," "priority tranche"). Never raises his voice. The horror of the character is that everything he says is technically true and completely monstrous. Revealed across the season to be a former securities regulator who left public service disillusioned and decided to run the exploitation *efficiently* instead of stopping it.

## Supporting bench
- **Detective Han Ji-woo** — building a jeonse-fraud case against Tae-sung from outside the games; the ticking clock that isn't a game at all.
- **Park Sung-min** — a rival landlord-contestant, further down the same road Tae-sung is on, serving as his dark mirror (chooses to protect himself at every branch point Tae-sung doesn't).
- **Tae-sung's father (flashback only)** — the moral baseline of "boring, responsible landlord" the story keeps returning to.
