# Themes & Visual/Tonal Direction

## Themes
- **Trust as leverage.** Jeonse only works because tenants trust landlords not to gamble with money that was never really "given." Every game mechanic re-stages that trust as a formal bet.
- **Politeness as violence.** Director Baek and the Office's entire aesthetic is corporate courtesy — the horror is bureaucratic, not gory.
- **The gap between "protected on paper" and "protected in time."** Every tenant in the games has a real, legally recognized claim — the show is explicit that Korean tenant law isn't toothless. What it's not is fast, forgiving of informal trust, or built for a building that's already underwater. The Office doesn't prey on ignorance so much as on latency and edge cases, which is a more indicting critique of the system than "people don't know their rights."
- **Restitution vs. redemption.** The story is deliberately suspicious of Tae-sung "winning back" the money as a clean redemption arc — Season 3 insists that real repair is slower and less satisfying than a game with a scoreboard.

## Visual / tonal notes
- Contrast the **officetel building's** warm, lived-in linework (soft color, hand-drawn texture) against the Office's game spaces (cold gradients, hard geometric panel grids) — visually marking when we've left "real life" and entered the system exploiting it.
- Avoid designing games as spectacle-first (no arena crowds, no flashy production) — keep them intimate, procedural, almost administrative, so the mundanity of Director Baek's world is the source of dread rather than scale.
- Tenant character designs should visibly age/wear across the season (Mi-jin's exhaustion, Halmoni Seo's hands) — cost is physical, not just financial.
