# Clients & Narrative

Clients are the point of the game. The reading is the verb; the clients are the reason to care about
getting it right — or getting it *wrong on purpose*.

## The client model

Every client is a **hand-authored puzzle** (see [`reading.md`](./reading.md)), built from roughly
these parts (intent, not a data schema):

- **Identity & presentation** — who they are and how they come across at the table, including the
  **appearance** the player can read and the **ID/wallet** they hand over (see
  [`instruments/observation.md`](./instruments/observation.md),
  [`instruments/identity.md`](./instruments/identity.md)).
- **Presenting concern** — the question or worry they walk in with and state out loud.
- **True need** — what they actually need, which may differ from the presenting concern. *"Sometimes
  the advice the client needs to hear isn't the accurate reading"* lives in the gap between presenting
  concern, true need, and the correct reading.
- **The predetermined reading** — their hard-set tarot spread and the **correct synthesis** the
  instruments cohere into. The solvable puzzle.
- **Instruments in play** — which of the toolkit (tarot, star chart, palmistry, observation,
  identity) this client's puzzle expects the player to lean on.
- **Backstory** — context that may surface over time, especially for recurring clients.

## One-off clients vs. arc clients

- **One-off clients** appear for a single self-contained visit. They give the player low-stakes
  reps on the mechanics and texture the world. Their outcome may be a small beat rather than a full
  arc.
- **Arc clients** are a **small, authored handful** whose storylines span the entire length of the
  game. They return across multiple days, remember what you told them, and change based on it. These
  carry the emotional weight.

> **Requirement:** the population each day is deliberately composed of both kinds. One-offs keep the
> loop varied and teach the methods; arc clients make choices matter over time.

## Memory & consequence

This is non-negotiable for the concept to work:

- **Recurring clients remember.** When an arc client returns, their state reflects the advice the
  player previously gave them. Their situation has moved — better, worse, sideways — as a result.
- **Advice ripples forward.** A choice made on Day 2 can be the setup for what a client brings back
  on Day 5. Consequences are often *delayed and revealed later*, not scored on the spot.
- **Branching, not a single track.** Different advice should be able to send an arc client's story
  down genuinely different paths, including toward different endings.

## Happy endings and "not needing you"

The narrative win condition is distinctive and worth stating plainly:

- The Story pushes the player to help clients toward **happy endings**.
- The deepest form of success is getting a client to a place where they **no longer need the
  psychic** — they've found their footing, made their decision, stopped outsourcing their life to
  fortune-telling. The business "losing" a client this way is a narrative victory.
- This gives the protagonist — the disbeliever who profits from belief — a quietly redemptive
  throughline: the best thing he can do is talk people *out* of needing him.

> **Open design question:** how the game frames a client leaving for good — bittersweet, triumphant,
> comedic, or player's-choice — is undecided. See [`open-questions.md`](./open-questions.md).

## The protagonist's own arc (candidate)

The pitch centers the clients, but the protagonist is a character too: a skeptic running a belief
business. Whether he has his own arc — does helping people change him? does he stay a cynic who
happens to do good? — is an open question worth deciding, because it shapes tone and ending.

## What the narrative system must support

- Clients that carry **state across days**.
- A way to author **multi-day arcs** for a handful of clients while dropping in **one-offs** around
  them.
- Advice choices that **branch** an arc client's trajectory.
- **Deferred consequences** — outcomes that surface days after the choice that caused them.
- Endings that can register a client as **helped / unchanged / harmed**, including the "no longer
  needs you" resolution.
